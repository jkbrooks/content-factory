# The Principal Delegation Model

When a principal can express intent and then step away — trusting that managers will interpret and execute without constant re-engagement — they operate at full leverage. The key is that interpretation burden lives with the manager, not the principal.

This requires three things:

1. **Accumulated context** — managers must already know enough about the principal's preferences to make judgment calls without asking.
2. **Clear escalation criteria** — workers know when to ask managers, managers know when to ask the principal. Most things never reach the principal.
3. **Distributed quality judgment** — the principal reviews output, not process. Someone else can recognize "done."

The failure mode in early-stage AI agent setups is that the AI acts as a flat organization: every decision escalates directly to the human because there's no middle layer with accumulated context. The fix is building that context layer explicitly — through persistent memory, documented preferences, and iterative trust calibration.

The interesting question is whether AI agents can fill the manager role over time. Not as a general-purpose chatbot, but as a specialized interpreter: one that has read enough of your prior decisions that it can translate "I want X" into an executable plan without clarification.

This is the design goal of systems like Paperclip's heartbeat-based org chart model — not just task execution, but goal-aware delegation where each agent understands why it's doing what it's doing.
